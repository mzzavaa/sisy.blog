Most speaker bios are written for the wrong audience.

They list credentials. Job titles. Years of experience. Companies worked at. They read like a LinkedIn summary — which is fine for LinkedIn, but catastrophic for a speaker bio that needs to answer one question in five seconds: **why should I book this person for my audience?**

## The organizer's actual question

When an event organizer reads your bio, they're not asking "is she impressive?" They're asking: *what will my audience get from hearing her speak?*

That's a completely different question — and most bios don't answer it at all.

## The SISY bio framework

A bio that books looks like this:

**[Who you help] + [What transformation you create] + [Why you're credible to deliver it]**

In practice:

> "Nadia helps engineering teams build AI systems they can actually defend — ethically, legally, and technically. A machine learning researcher turned practitioner, she's led AI governance programmes at three European banks and now advises regulators on responsible deployment standards."

Notice what's missing: no job title, no list of companies, no years of experience. What's there instead: a clear audience, a clear outcome, and proof she can deliver it.

## The three-sentence structure

Write your bio in exactly three sentences:

1. **The value sentence**: Who you help and what outcome you create.
2. **The credibility sentence**: One or two specific, concrete things that prove you can deliver that outcome.
3. **The context sentence**: What you're doing now that's relevant to this stage.

Keep each sentence to 25 words or fewer. If you can't say it in 25 words, you haven't decided what you actually want to say.

## Common mistakes to cut immediately

- **"Passionate about"** — everyone is passionate. Cut it.
- **"X years of experience"** — experience doesn't equal expertise. Replace with a specific outcome.
- **Your employer's name in the first sentence** — the audience is there for you, not your company.
- **Third-person that reads like it was written about someone else** — it was. Rewrite it like you wrote it yourself.

## Your action for today

Open your current bio. Find the first sentence. Ask: does this tell an organiser what their audience will gain? If not, rewrite it using the value sentence formula above. Then read the whole thing out loud. If it sounds like a CV, it needs more work.

You have everything you need to write a bio that books. The only thing standing between you and it is an hour this afternoon.

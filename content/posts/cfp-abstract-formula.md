Organizers read hundreds of CFP submissions. Most get rejected in the first paragraph.

Not because the topic isn't interesting. Not because the speaker isn't qualified. Because the abstract doesn't answer the question the organizer is actually asking.

## The question every organizer is asking

**"What will my attendees be able to do differently after this session?"**

Not "what will they know." Not "what will they have heard." What will they *do* differently?

Most CFP abstracts answer the wrong question. They describe the speaker's expertise, or the topic's importance, or the speaker's enthusiasm for the subject. None of that answers the organizer's question.

## The formula

Accepted CFP abstracts follow this structure:

1. **The problem** (1–2 sentences): Name the specific pain your audience is feeling right now.
2. **The promise** (1 sentence): What they'll be able to do after your session that they can't do now.
3. **The path** (2–3 sentences): The key steps or insights you'll share to get them there.
4. **The proof** (1 sentence): One concrete signal that you've done this and it worked.

Total: 6–8 sentences. No more.

## An example

**Before (rejected):**
> "In this talk, I'll share my experience working on AI ethics at a major European bank, covering the challenges we faced implementing responsible AI frameworks and what I learned about organizational change management in regulated industries."

**After (accepted):**
> "Most AI teams know their model has problems — they just don't know how to document them in a way that satisfies regulators without freezing the entire project. In this session, you'll leave with a one-page model card template that has passed legal review at three regulated institutions. We'll walk through the five fields that matter most to auditors, the two that teams always overcomplicate, and how to get sign-off from legal in one round instead of six. I've used this exact template to move two AI deployments from 'blocked' to 'approved' in under a month."

Same speaker. Same expertise. Completely different result.

## The most common mistake

Leading with "In this talk, I will..."

Those five words signal to organizers that the abstract is about you — not about them or their audience. Cut them every time. Start with the problem or the audience instead.

## Apply it now

Take your current CFP abstract. Find the first sentence. Does it name a problem your audience is feeling, or does it describe you and your talk? If it's the latter, rewrite the opening sentence to name the pain first. Then check: does your abstract end with proof you can deliver what you're promising?

Those two changes alone will put your submission ahead of 80% of the pile.
